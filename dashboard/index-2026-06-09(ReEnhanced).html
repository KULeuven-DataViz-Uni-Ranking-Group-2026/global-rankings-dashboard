2 · THE vs QS

Does it matter which ranking system you trust?

This chart uses percentile-position disagreement, not raw rank difference. It shows where THE and QS produce different views of the same institutions.

Rank is system-dependent A university's position changes depending on whether THE or QS is used.
Volatility is uneven Some regions experience larger THE-QS percentile disagreement than others.
Methodology is strategy Universities can rise in one system while remaining less visible in another.

Policy insight

Rankings are not neutral mirrors of quality. They are measurement systems with different priorities. Strategic planners should therefore choose which ranking system they are optimising for, instead of treating global rank as a single objective fact.

3 · Elite Stability

Can any university break into the global top 5?

The top universities do not move like ordinary institutions. Their rank ranges are narrow, showing a highly stable prestige ceiling.

The top is sticky Elite universities remain within a narrow rank range across the decade.
Top-5 access is difficult The highest positions are dominated by a small group of historically prestigious institutions.
Better target zone For most systems, the Top 20-50 is a more realistic strategic intervention zone.

Policy insight

For non-Anglosphere systems, aiming immediately for the global Top 5 may be inefficient. The data suggests that the pinnacle is structurally stable. Policy should instead focus on moving institutions into the more volatile elite boundary zones.

Policy insight

THE operationalises excellence mainly through research capacity. This is a methodology audit, not a causal model. It does not mean teaching or industry engagement are unimportant, but it does mean that they are weaker levers for improving THE rank compared with research strength.

Policy insight

Subject strategy is ranking strategy. Asia shows the clearest Engineering-oriented profile (+156 median rank places). The United States (+2), United Kingdom (-7), and other Anglosphere systems (+13) are close to balanced, suggesting comprehensive university models. Europe (-162) and the Rest-of-World group (-350) show relatively stronger Arts & Humanities positions. For policymakers, this means STEM specialisation can be a powerful route to ranking visibility, but it is not the only institutional strategy.

Epilogue

Final recommendation

Policymakers should not treat global rank as a single neutral truth. Global rankings reward different routes to visibility: historical prestige, research capacity, national investment, and subject specialisation. A responsible ranking strategy should triangulate multiple systems, choose which mission and ranking logic to optimise for, and avoid reducing university quality to a narrow STEM-only agenda.
